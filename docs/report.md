# ProveKit Prover Profiling Report: `complete_age_check` Circuit

> Rebuilt from source (`cargo build --release`), profiled with macOS `sample` (1 kHz, 3 s),
> `samply record`, and ProveKit's built-in `SpanStats` tracing layer.
> Flamegraph: `docs/flamegraphs/flamegraph_prove.svg`
> Analysis script: `docs/analyze_flamegraph.py`
> Machine: Apple Silicon (arm64), 16 cores, 48 GB RAM, rustc 1.96.0-nightly.

---

## 1. Circuit Overview

The `complete_age_check` circuit is a monolithic Noir passport verification program that:
- Verifies the holder's age against min/max bounds
- Checks passport expiry against the current date
- Verifies DG1 hash integrity within the SOD (Security Object of the Document)
- Validates the full certificate chain (DSC signed by CSC) using 2048-bit and 4096-bit RSA

After compilation and R1CS optimization (Gaussian elimination removes ~11.4% of constraints):
- **631,480 constraints** (down from 712,452)
- **1,248,358 witnesses** (split: w1 = 621,295, w2 = 627,063)
- R1CS matrices: A has 1,808,881 entries, B has 715,169, C has 1,073,534

### 3.4 Inside `inner_blinded_prove` — The WHIR Round Structure

Each WHIR proof runs 5 rounds of the FRI-like folding protocol. Each round consists of:
1. **Sumcheck** — proving polynomial identity over the boolean hypercube
2. **IRS Commit** — Reed-Solomon encoding (NTT) + Merkle tree commitment
3. **IRS Open** — Merkle path opening for queried positions
4. **Proof of Work** — lightweight PoW for Fiat-Shamir soundness

### 4.3 Key Insight: `Fr::mul_assign` Dominates — But NOT in Skyscraper

The single function `<MontBackend<FrConfig, 4> as FpConfig<4>>::mul_assign` accounts for **28.1% of all CPU samples**. This is the 4-limb Montgomery multiplication for the BN254 scalar field (254-bit prime), from the `ark_ff` library.

Programmatic analysis of the collapsed flamegraph stacks (`docs/analyze_flamegraph.py`) reveals that `mul_assign` self-time equals its inclusive time (ratio = 1.00) — it is always a leaf function. Its **direct callers**, grouped by subsystem:

| Subsystem | mul_assign samples | % of all mul_assign | What calls it |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|---------------|
| **NTT (Cooley-Tukey)** | 1,546 | **72.3%** | `apply_twiddles`, `NttEngine::ntt_dispatch` |
| **mixed_dot (weight folding)** | 252 | **11.8%** | `whir::mixed_dot` |
| **geometric_accumulate** | 184 | **8.6%** | `whir::geometric_accumulate` |
| **Sumcheck prover** | 92 | **4.3%** | `compute_sumcheck_polynomial`, `sumcheck::fold` |
| **evaluate_gamma_block** | 25 | **1.2%** | Rayon parallel gamma evaluation |
| **Other** | 39 | **1.8%** | `mixed_univariate_evaluate`, witness gen, etc. |

**Critical finding: Skyscraper hash does NOT call `ark_ff::mul_assign`.** Skyscraper uses its own Montgomery squaring implementation (`bn254_multiplier::montgomery_square_log_interleaved_4`) which is a separate ARM NEON SIMD routine in the `skyscraper/bn254-multiplier` crate. The 19.0% of CPU time spent in Skyscraper is entirely independent of `ark_ff`'s field multiplication.

This means the NTT is an even more dominant consumer of `ark_ff::mul_assign` than it first appears — **nearly three-quarters of all field multiplications happen inside NTT butterfly operations**.

## 5. The Most Fundamental Operations in the Proving Flow

Ranked by measured contribution to total CPU time (from flamegraph sampling):

### Tier 1: Dominant (>10% each)

1. **BN254 Montgomery Multiplication** (28.1% self-time)
   The atomic arithmetic operation. A 4-limb Montgomery multiply on a 254-bit prime field element. Every polynomial operation, every hash reduction, every dot product bottlenecks here. The implementation uses `ark_bn254::Fr` with `ark_ff`'s generic Montgomery backend. On Apple Silicon, each mul takes ~15-20 ns.

2. **Skyscraper Hash Compression** (19.0% inclusive)
   The Merkle tree hash function. Each call to `compress_many` processes 64-byte message blocks through: (a) a byte-level S-box (`sbox`: bitwise rotate + AND + XOR), (b) a Montgomery squaring via `bn254_multiplier::montgomery_square_log_interleaved_4` using ARM NEON SIMD, and (c) modular reduction via `reduce_partial`. The S-box is NOT field-arithmetic-bound — it operates on individual bytes. The squaring uses a **separate SIMD Montgomery implementation** in the `bn254-multiplier` crate, completely independent of `ark_ff::mul_assign`. This is why Skyscraper contributes 0% of `ark_ff::mul_assign` samples despite being 19% of total CPU time.

3. **NTT / Reed-Solomon Encoding** (15.2% + 5.3% transpose = 20.5% inclusive)
   The Cooley-Tukey FFT over BN254 Fr. Used in every IRS Commit phase to evaluate the witness polynomial over a larger domain. The NTT itself (`ntt_dispatch`) is 8.3% self-time, but the data layout transpositions (`transpose_copy` 3.6%, `transpose` 1.7%) add significant memory-bound overhead. The largest NTT operates on 1M-element polynomials (32 MB of field elements).

### Tier 2: Significant (3-10% each)

4. **Weight Folding / mixed_dot** (6.1%)
   Dense dot products between weight vectors and polynomial evaluations during the ZK blinding layer. The `fold_weight_to_mask_size` function runs 5 times per proof (once per WHIR round), each time folding vectors of up to 1M elements. Pure field multiply-accumulate.

5. **Sumcheck Prover** (5.5%)
   The interactive sumcheck protocol. The hot loop in `sumcheck_fold_map_reduce_inner` evaluates the sumcheck polynomial at 3 points per variable, using the `map` closure that computes cubic polynomial evaluations. Each evaluation involves 3 field multiplications and 3 additions. Parallelized via Rayon with recursive splitting.

6. **XZ/LZMA Decompression** (5.5%)
   Reading and decompressing the prover key (4.6 MB XZ-compressed → ~580 MB in memory). This is pure I/O overhead, not proving computation. The `lzma_decode` function alone is 2.9% self-time.

### Tier 3: Notable (1-3% each)

7. **evaluate_gamma_block** (1.4%) — ZK blinding gamma polynomial evaluation, parallelized via Rayon fold-map-reduce.

8. **geometric_accumulate** (1.3%) — Computing geometric series Σ αⁱ for WHIR round challenges.

9. **Sparse Matrix Operations** (2.4%) — R1CS matrix-vector products (A·w, B·w) and matrix transpose for the sumcheck. Includes parallel sort for CSR→CSC conversion.

10. **Matrix Commit Encoding** (0.6%) — `ArkFieldEncoder::encode` converts field elements to bytes for Merkle tree leaf hashing.

### Tier 4: Negligible (<1%)

11. **Witness Generation** (0.6%) — Noir ACVM interpreter executing the circuit. Surprisingly cheap because the ACVM is a simple bytecode VM and the circuit's RSA/SHA operations are implemented as blackbox opcodes.

12. **Proof Serialization** (0.7%) — Zstd compression of the 3.3 MB proof.

## 6. Field Multiplication Impact Analysis — Programmatically Verified

**Question: By how much would proving time decrease if field multiplication took half as long?**

### Methodology

Rather than estimating fractions by hand, the impact was computed programmatically by parsing all 7,600 flamegraph samples (`docs/analyze_flamegraph.py`). The script:

1. Identifies every sample where `ark_ff::mul_assign` is the leaf function (self-time)
2. Traces the direct caller of `mul_assign` in each stack to attribute it to a subsystem
3. Applies Amdahl's Law: halving `mul_assign` saves exactly half its self-time samples
4. Maps sample counts to wall-clock time using SpanStats calibration

### Raw Data (from `analyze_flamegraph.py`)

```
Total samples:                7,600
mul_assign self-time:         2,138 samples (28.1%)
mul_assign inclusive:          2,138 samples (28.1%)  ← ratio 1.00, always a leaf
Overhead (dyld+LZMA+kernel):  1,369 samples (18.0%)
Proving-only samples:          6,231
```

### Where mul_assign Is Called From (exact, from stack traces)

| Caller subsystem | Samples | % of mul_assign |
|-----------------|---------|-----------------|
| NTT (Cooley-Tukey butterflies) | 1,546 | 72.3% |
| mixed_dot (weight folding) | 252 | 11.8% |
| geometric_accumulate | 184 | 8.6% |
| Sumcheck prover | 92 | 4.3% |
| evaluate_gamma_block | 25 | 1.2% |
| Other (univariate eval, witness gen) | 39 | 1.8% |
| **Skyscraper hash** | **0** | **0.0%** |

**Skyscraper uses its own `bn254_multiplier::montgomery_square_log_interleaved_4` (ARM NEON SIMD), NOT `ark_ff::mul_assign`.** This is a critical distinction: the 19% of CPU time in Skyscraper would be completely unaffected by speeding up `ark_ff` field multiplication.

### Computed Speedup (3 methods)

**Method 1: Amdahl's Law on self-time (upper bound for total prove command)**

```
mul_assign self-time:    2,138 samples
Samples saved (÷2):     1,069
Total samples:           7,600
Time reduction:          14.1%
Speedup:                 1.164×
```

**Method 2: Proving-only (excluding I/O + OS overhead)**

```
Overhead samples:        1,369 (dyld, LZMA, kernel context switches)
Proving-only samples:    6,231
Samples saved:           1,069
Proving time reduction:  17.2%
Proving speedup:         1.207×
```

**Method 3: Wall-clock mapping (SpanStats-calibrated)**

```
prove_with_witness:      1,400 ms  (from SpanStats)
I/O overhead:              740 ms  (key deser + proof write)
mul_assign % of proving:  34.3%    (2,138 / 6,231)
mul_assign time:           480 ms
Time saved (÷2):           240 ms
New prove_with_witness:  1,160 ms
New total prove command: 1,900 ms
Proving speedup:         1.207×
End-to-end speedup:      1.126×
```

### Cross-Validation

If `mul_assign` were **infinitely fast** (0 ns, not just 2× faster):

```
Max samples saved:       2,138
Max proving reduction:   34.3%
Max proving speedup:     1.522×
Max end-to-end speedup:  1.391×
```

This sets the theoretical ceiling: even with perfect field multiplication, proving can be at most 1.52× faster. The remaining 65.7% of proving time is in Skyscraper hashing (separate SIMD mul), NTT transposes (memory-bound), sumcheck non-mul work, and parallelism overhead.

### Verdict

A 2× `ark_ff::mul_assign` speedup yields:
- **1.207× proving speedup** (17.2% reduction, saving ~240 ms off 1,400 ms)
- **1.126× end-to-end speedup** (14.1% reduction, saving ~240 ms off 2,140 ms)

This is **significant** — a quarter-second off a 1.4-second prove is noticeable. But it is **not transformative** because:

1. **NTT consumes 72.3% of all `mul_assign` calls**, but the NTT also has substantial non-mul overhead (5.3% in memory transposes). Halving mul doesn't help the transpose.
2. **Skyscraper (19% of CPU) is completely unaffected** — it uses its own SIMD Montgomery implementation.
3. **OS/parallelism overhead (4.9%) is fixed** — Rayon context switches don't depend on arithmetic speed.
4. **I/O (5.5% LZMA + 4.3% dyld) is fixed** — prover key decompression is independent.

### More Impactful Optimization Targets

From the flamegraph, ranked by potential impact:

1. **SIMD-vectorized NTT for BN254 on ARM NEON** — NTT + transpose = 20.5% of time, and 72.3% of all `mul_assign` calls. A vectorized radix-4 NTT with fused twiddle application could reduce both the mul and transpose costs simultaneously. Potential: 2-3× NTT speedup → 10-15% total proving reduction.

2. **Skyscraper S-box vectorization** — The S-box in `bar()` operates on individual bytes with scalar bitwise ops. NEON `TBL`/`TBX` byte-shuffle instructions could vectorize the 32-byte S-box application. The Montgomery squaring is already SIMD. Potential: 1.5× Skyscraper speedup → 6% total reduction.

3. **Reducing WHIR round count** — Each of the 5 rounds adds NTT + Merkle + sumcheck. Going from 5 to 4 rounds (with adjusted security parameters) would save ~20% of `inner_blinded_prove` time. Potential: 5-8% total proving reduction.

4. **Fusing weight-folding + gamma-evaluation** — `zk_w_folded_compute` (5.6%) and `evaluate_gamma_block` (9.8%) iterate over the same polynomial data. Fusing them into a single pass would improve L2 cache hit rates on the 32 MB witness polynomial.

## 9. Architectural Observations

### The WHIR protocol structure drives the cost profile

ProveKit uses the WHIR (Worst-case to average-case reduction for Hashing-based Interactive oracle proofs over Reed-solomon codes) protocol for polynomial commitments. The proving cost is dominated by:

1. **Two full WHIR ZK proofs** (one per witness split w1/w2), each running 5 FRI-like rounds
2. **Each round** performs NTT → Merkle commit → sumcheck → open, with geometrically decreasing domain sizes
3. The **first round** of each proof is the most expensive (domain size 1M), accounting for ~50% of that proof's time

### The dual-commitment architecture

The witness is split into w1 (621,295 elements, pre-challenge) and w2 (627,063 elements, post-challenge). This split enables a challenge–response pattern for LogUp-based lookups but doubles the commitment and WHIR proof work. Circuits without lookup tables would only need a single commitment, roughly halving the proving time.

### Skyscraper hash is well-suited but not free

The Skyscraper hash function accounts for 19% of total CPU time. It uses a custom S-box operating on individual bytes (bitwise rotate + AND + XOR) followed by Montgomery squaring via `bn254_multiplier::montgomery_square_log_interleaved_4`, which uses ARM NEON SIMD. The largest Merkle tree (524,288 leaves) commits in ~11 ms. While this is competitive with SHA-256 for Merkle hashing, the flamegraph shows it is the second-largest time consumer after field multiplication.

### NTT is memory-bound, not just compute-bound

The NTT accounts for 20.5% of time (15.2% compute + 5.3% transpose). The transpose operations are pure memory movement, shuffling 32 MB of field elements between row-major and column-major layouts. This means faster field arithmetic alone will not fully accelerate the NTT; cache-friendly data layouts and prefetching matter too.

### Rayon parallelism has measurable overhead

The 4.9% kernel overhead (context switches, mutex waits, ulock) is the cost of Rayon's work-stealing thread pool. With 16 cores the parallel speedup is substantial but not free. Both the sumcheck and NTT use recursive parallel splitting, which generates many small tasks.

## 10. Summary

The `complete_age_check` circuit proves in **~1.40 s** of actual computation (2.14 s wall-clock including I/O). The flamegraph reveals that **BN254 Montgomery multiplication (`ark_ff::mul_assign`) is the single hottest function at 28.1% of CPU time**, followed by Skyscraper hashing (19.0%) and NTT (20.5% including transposes).

Programmatic analysis of 7,600 flamegraph samples (`docs/analyze_flamegraph.py`) shows that **72.3% of all `mul_assign` calls originate from NTT butterfly operations**, while **Skyscraper uses a completely separate SIMD Montgomery implementation** (`bn254_multiplier`) and would be unaffected by `ark_ff` speedups.

Halving `ark_ff::mul_assign` time yields a programmatically verified **1.207× proving speedup (17.2% reduction, saving ~240 ms)** and **1.126× end-to-end speedup**. Even making `mul_assign` infinitely fast caps the proving speedup at 1.52× — the remaining 65.7% of proving time is in Skyscraper SIMD hashing, NTT memory transposes, and parallelism overhead.

The most promising optimization vectors are: (1) SIMD-vectorized NTT for BN254 on ARM NEON (targets 72.3% of all field muls + 5.3% transpose overhead), (2) Skyscraper S-box vectorization with NEON byte-shuffle instructions, (3) reducing the WHIR round count from 5 to 4, and (4) fusing the weight-folding and gamma-evaluation passes for better cache locality.
